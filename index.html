Cost curves

Technological costs are rarely static over time. Costs can fall when we scale up the production of some technology for two main reasons. The first is economies of scale. This simply describes how it can often be cheaper (per unit) to produce more of something than less of something. If you have a factory Learning rates are used to describe how much cheaper a technology gets every year or after a set amount of cumulative ouptut. They are helpful when thinking about the tragectory the price of a technology may take over time as it benefits from economies of scale & also from greater experience in making the technology.

We can typically describe the learning curve of a technology by the % amount cheaper it gets to produce each year. So for example if a technology cost 1000 dollars today and had a learning rate of 20%, then this means that every year we might expect the technology to get 20% cheaper. So after 1 year with this learning rate we might expect the technology to cost 800 dollars, after 2 years, 640 dollars, and after 10 years 107 dollars.

Wright's law

The examples above use a learning rate that takes effect over time, each year some technology gets x% cheaper. While this has a descriptive property and can be helpful for checking if we are on the right kind of trajectory it mainly is just that, descriptive. It would be a bit weird for us to suggest there is a direct causal relationship between the simple passage of time and things getting chepaer, in reality people have to be working at making things better.

This is where Wright's law comes in. This is is similar to Moore's law, or the cost curves above, in the sense that it tracks cost decreases, but, instead of looking at them as a function of time it looks at them as a function of quantity produced. So instead of how much cheaper do transistors get every year you would look at how much cheaper transistors get when you have made double the previous quantity. The 'learning rate' is the % costs fall for a doubling of cumulative ouput.

If we want to turn this into an equation to predict future costs then we can use \(C_x = C_1 x^{log_2(b)} \) where \(C_x \) is the cost of the \(x^{th} \) unit, \(C_1 \) is the cost of the \(1^{st} \) unit and \(b = 1- l \) where \( l \) is the learning rate. So for example if costs decrease 20% for every cumulative doubling of output and our first unit cost 500 dollars then \(l = 0.2 \), \(C_1 = 500 \) and we get \(C_x = 500 x^{log_2(0.8)} \).

Portfolio of approaches

One big simplification used up until this point is to treat 'carbon removal' as an almost homogeneous group that has a single cost curve. In reality there are many different technologies all capable of removing CO₂ from the atmosphere and they all exhibit differences in how large the can likely scale to, how quickly they can scale, how expensive they are today and how much we might expect them to get cheaper in the future (cost curves). For example, as far as different sources of carbon removal go there are:

  1. Forests
  2. Direct Air Capture
  3. Bioenergy with carbon capture and storage
  4. Soil
  5. Blue Carbon
  6. Enhanced Weathering

These all come with different tradeoffs, for example while forests can remove a tonne of carbon much more cheaply than many alternatives if you want to scale them up you run up against actual available land quantities. DAC doesn't take up much land at all but is currently one of the more expensive options and also requires a lot of energy.

The more technological solutions, while typically the more expensive options today are also the ones that are the most likely to benefit from learning effects helping to reduce their costs over time. Because of these difference in learning rates it doesn't necessarily make sense to put all our money into what is today the cheapest carbon removal options if this means forgoing the chance to build capacity in the sources that might be the best in the longer run.

Support

While not everyone will be able to get directly involved in this work there are still things anyone can do to help out to varying degrees.

Firstly, carbon removal has had a history of being a somewhat controversial topic and so hopefully what you've learnt here will help to pursuade you of the undeniable need now for it to be part of any climate solution.

Secondly, if you want to get more supportive you could consider donating to Our World in Data or Stripe Climate. While Our World in Data does not specifically focus on carbon removal it is an absolutly essential effort to help make the worlds data, including energy and emissions data, as widely visible as possible and so is a hugly beneficial resource for researchers, the media, and everyone else. Stripe Climate helps carbon removal companies by being their buyer of first resort, allowing them survive at their high current cost of production until they have moved down their cost curves to a more competitive and sustainable level. You can donate as an individual or, if you are an online business, you can directly contribute a percentage of your revenue to the programme.
