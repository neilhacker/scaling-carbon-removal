A cost curve is a way of looking at the unit cost of a technology and how this changes as we scale up the production of this technology. Typically this involves describing the % decrease in unit price for a doubling in the cumulative volume of this technology, we then call this % decrease in cost the learning rate. For example if a technology gets 20% cheaper every time we double the cumulative volume of units made then it has a learning rate of 20%. Portfolio approach

One large simplification we've been using up until now is that there will be a single, constant, learning curve. This has the effect of assuming almost all technologies can be treated alike. This is very much not the case, some might be cheaper today so look more attractive for those who want immediate cost effective ways of removing CO₂. Some might have a lower learning rate so we won't expect them to fall in price by as much as we scale volume. Some may have stricter limits on how much they can actually scale, such as running out of the right kind of land. Some may be able to fall in price but may have a harder to budge price floor due to things like energy use.

Considering every trade off is more than I want to go into in this piece, if you do want a really great look at how these things effect more specifically what you want to use for CO₂ removal I'd really recommend checking out roadTo10gigatons.com. What I want to do here are look at a few simplified examples to give some intuition as to why, at this stage, spreading funding out over a portfolio of options may well be the optimal move.

Firstly let's look at a case where we have different learning rates, with a currently high cost option also having a high learning rate. This means that as we scale this technology even though it costs a lot now we might expect it to get a lot cheaper in the future. While this might not be the case in real life to make the comparison simple we will assume both technologies have removed the same cumulative amount of CO₂ so far and so start at the same quantity value on their cost curves.

Technology A has a low current cost per tonne of $75 to remove 10000 tonnes but it also has a very low learning rate of 2% i.e it won't see much decrease from its current cost level as use of it scales up.
Technology B however is starting from a higher current cost per tonne of $500 to remove 10000 tonnes but also has a higher learning rate of 20% . This means that it will see steeper declines in cost if we scale up its usage.

Uncertainty

There is a famous probability puzzle called the multi-armed bandit problem. In it we are faced with a number of one armed bandits (slot machines) all with a different probability of paying out the jackpot. We want to maximise our expected winnings over a certain number of arm pulls. The problem though is that we don't know what the likihood of any machine to pay out is. If we want to learn this we have to spend some of our arm pulls to get a better idea of how likely a given machine is to pay out, but if we only have a set number of total arm pulls what is our best course of action?
