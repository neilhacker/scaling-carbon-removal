Cost curves

Learning rates are used to describe how much cheaper a technology gets every year or after a set amount of cumulative ouptut. They are helpful when thinking about the tragectory the price of a technology may take over time as it benefits from economies of scale & also from greater experience in making the technology.

We can typically describe the learning curve of a technology by the % amount cheaper it gets to produce each year. So for example if a technology cost 1000 dollars today and had a learning rate of 20%, then this means that every year we might expect the technology to get 20% cheaper. So after 1 year with this learning rate we might expect the technology to cost 800 dollars, after 2 years, 640 dollars, and after 10 years 107 dollars.

Support

While not everyone will be able to get directly involved in this work there are still things anyone can do to help out to varying degrees.

Firstly, carbon removal has had a history of being a somewhat controversial topic and so hopefully what you've learnt here will help to pursuade you of the undeniable need now for it to be part of any climate solution.

Secondly, if you want to get more supportive you could consider donating to Our World in Data or Stripe Climate. While Our World in Data does not specifically focus on carbon removal it is an absolutly essential effort to help make the worlds data, including energy and emissions data, as widely visible as possible and so is a hugly beneficial resource for researchers, the media, and everyone else. Stripe Climate helps carbon removal companies by being their buyer of first resort, allowing them survive at their high current cost of production until they have moved down their cost curves to a more competitive and sustainable level. You can donate as an individual or, if you are an online business, you can directly contribute a percentage of your revenue to the programme.
